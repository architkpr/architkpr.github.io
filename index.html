Intrusion Detection

Intrusion Detection using Active Authentication

During my spring semester at Columbia University, I took up the Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) course. One of the interesting projects I worked on was implementing an IDS using active authentication. This project worked on a simple idea that different people have different ways of using their computer, and that this difference in usage behavior can help us validate the identity of the user.

Maybe you have several tabs open simultaneously when working on your laptop. Maybe you use 3 different browsers, while constantly running Spotify in the background, and working on Word/Excel documents. Maybe you only use your laptop in the afternoon, or use it late at night to watch your favorite movies. For an intruder to work on your system without getting reported, they would have to replicate this behaviour which is challenging unless they have observed your laptop usage behavior over a long period of time.

The project involved gathering network and application usage data for over 4 days using audit tools available on the system. Relevant features were then extracted from the data and used to train a Machine Learning model, testing it over a few hours of use by an intruder. Due to time constraints, the testing and training phases were of short durations. However, the model detected an intruder with about 85% true and 20% false positive ratings.

Adaptive Streaming

Adaptive Bitrate Streaming of Video Content

During fall of 2019 I took up the Computer Networks course at Columbia University. An exciting project I worked on was studying how services like Netflix provide video content to its users, by implementing a HTTP proxy server that performs adaptive bitrate streaming of video content.

Gone are the days when video streaming would require you to pause the video and wait for it to buffer, before resuming your stream. With today’s networks making progress in terms of speed and reliability, content providers have moved to better networking methods to deliver their services. The concept of “Adaptive Streaming” allows Netflix servers to select the highest quality video frames that can be delivered over the network, depending on the available bandwidth at the time. In other words, if the internet load is high where you are trying to stream a video, the video wouldn’t pause and buffer, and instead would reduce its quality so you can get a smooth viewing experience.

My project involved implementing this system, by making use of socket programming in Python, receiving and parsing client/server requests and calculating network throughput to provide the best quality video experience without buffering.
